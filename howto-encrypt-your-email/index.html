Howto - Encrypt your email

Email security today is all about having a secure connection (TLS/SSL) when sending/receiving/checking email.

If you host your own mail server and that server get hacked, that connection security means no security at all. Your email lays there in plain text waiting for someone to read them.
What if someone hacks into your GMail account? Same thing.

What you can do is to encrypt your email body so you have to have the right key file or certificate to even read the email.

GPG or S/MIME

There are two spread types of email encryption, GPG and S/MIME. This post will be about S/MIME since it’s the easiest one. All major email clients support it out of the box. GPG often requires you to install extra software and thats not going to happen for the majority of people.

How does S/MIME work?

You generate a certificate for your mail address (can be done for free here). This gives you a private and a public cert. When you sign an outgoing email you pass your public cert along. When the receiver of that (unencrypted, but signed) email replies, his email client recognizes the cert and encrypts that email before sending it.
To decrypt the email one has to have the private certificate, which only you have. Your email client sees what email address the encrypted email is sent to and looks for a private key. When the email client finds the key, the email is decrypted.
This is done in the background and you as the user don’t see the encrypted text, ever. You see a symbol which tells you that it’s encrypted.

The only thing you have to think about it to sign (attach your public certificate) to your first outgoing email. If the receiver uses S/MIME everything is automated and transparent and they’ll encrypt and sign (attach their public certificate so you can encrypt your next reply) when replying to you.
If the receiver doesn’t use S/MIME they’ll see a attached file with a .p7s extension wondering what the hell that is.

Client support

Lots of clients support S/MIME out of the box. Microsoft Outlook, Outlook Express, Apple Mail, iOS Mail (iPhone/iPad), Android via additional apps, Thunderbird etc.

Howto

These steps are done on a Mac with OS X 10.8. On iOS, you have to import your private key by exporting it from your Keychain (.p12 file) and sending it to your device by email/dropbox/whatever and clicking on it to import. You also have to activate S/MIME on that email account in iOS.

  1. Get a certificate. You can do it here for free.
  2. Install it in your Keychain by double clicking on it. Restart Mail.app.
  3. Done. When you compose a new message you’ll have the option to sign the email (and encrypt if you’re replying to a signed one).
